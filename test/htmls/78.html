Inside N.Y.C.’s First-Ever Cereal Bar: 24 Options, 25 Toppings, and 5 Types of Milk

email
Facebook
Twitter
WhatsApp
  • David Graver

Ronnie Fieg might not be a household name, but most have seen the designer’s work on his acclaimed releases for Nike, Asics, New Balance, or myriad of products at either of his Kith storefront locations in New York City. Kith Brooklyn, his first retail spot, opened in 2011, has recently undergone a complete renovation at the hands of design studio Snarkitecture. And nestled inside the stark, futuristic 3,000-square-foot space is just what Brooklyn needed: a breakfast-cereal bar.

On the menu: 24 different flavors of cereal, five specialty milks (including hemp), and 25 assorted toppings. All of this can be custom ordered per one’s sugar-dusted wants and wishes—though a “Specials” menu provides suggested combinations for consumption. Crushed Oreos can top Apple Jacks and Frosted Flakes, with almond milk to tie it all together. Rice Krispies and Cinnamon Toast Crunch are calling for a flavor boost from mini-marshmallows (Lucky Charms marshmallows, in fact) and why not throw on some coconut flakes and a splash of chocolate milk? Everything comes individually packaged, something done on site, and guests assemble their cereal concoctions to their own liking. For the thicker “Swirl" option, ice cream replaces milk. It’s a nostalgic wonderland, a nutritionist’s nightmare, and a great lunch option if you’re in the neighborhood . . .

Within the store, Fieg’s eye for design touches everything, from the L.C.D. screens scrolling through cereal-box design history to the custom serving containers. For those ordering two or more portions of cereal, the mix is delivered in a cardboard mini-shoebox, designed in collaboration with Nike. In light of the forthcoming U.S. Open, Fieg and Nike utilized an iconic Andre Agassi image on the first round of boxes—reflective of the story told throughout the greater shop: the heated 90s rivalry between Agassi and Pete Sampras. Fieg plans on changing out the cereal box collaborations every six to eight weeks.

But where did the cereal x sneaker idea come from? “When I was younger, I wasn’t allowed to eat sugary cereals in my house,” said Fieg, “so I used to have to go to my friend’s house to sneak it in. I was obsessed. When I would go to school, my father used to make me lunch and I would sell it for a dollar so that I could go buy cereal. I used to eat Frosted Flakes with chocolate milk every day.” As young as 13, Fieg dreamed of having a cereal bar. He held onto the idea, seriously entertaining it once again in 2005. With the revamp of his Brooklyn outpost, it only made sense. “It was one of my earliest dreams, and I couldn’t miss out on the opportunity to make this happen.”

Fieg didn’t enter the culinary world alone, but partnered with childhood friend Danny Omari, one of the founders of bite-size cupcake brand Baked by Melissa‚ and the longtime owner of Cafe Bari, which closed last year. Omari also stocks La Colombe coffee and hot chocolate, altogether making it a full-stop breakfast spot.

This is Fieg’s second partnership with Snarkitecture—which has captured the art world’s collective consciousness right now by creating a “beach” of plastic balls within Washington, D.C.’s National Building Museum. In many ways, the herringbone floors, the black-and-white color scheme, and the ceiling of Air Jordan II sculptures hark back to his Soho location, but there’s something distinct here. “Daniel [Arsham, one of Snarkitecture’s founders] is about spaces in the way that I am about product, and we played off of that. It’s similar to what you are accustomed to seeing in Tokyo, it’s very detailed from the floor to the ceiling. This store is about creating moments and telling the story.”

Kith Treats is located at 233 Flatbush Avenue in Brooklyn, and will open to the public on Tuesday, August 25, 2015. The cereal bar opens at seven each morning, hours in advance of the rest of the space, but an archive lobby display and clear glass doors let patrons gaze into the further wonders of the space.
