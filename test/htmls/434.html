Benedict Cumberbatch’s Hamlet Plagued by Leaks from All Sides

email
Facebook
Twitter
WhatsApp
  • Joanna Robinson

Benedict Cumberbatch’s production of Hamlet is still technically in previews, but, as you might expect, history’s most famous play starring social media’s most beloved Brit has started with a bang, not a whimper.

The first issue plaguing the production came from the London media. Perhaps hoping to slake the thirst of the Cumberbitches and bros who couldn’t manage to snag tickets for the sold-out production, The Daily Mail, The Telegraph, and The Times all ran reviews of the first night of previews. This is a theater critic faux pas as reviews should technically be held until the play’s official opening night on August 25th. And while The Daily Mail and The Telegraph gave largely positive feedback, The Times came under attack for publishing quite a caustic, early review. Giving the production a paltry two stars, Times critic Kate Maltby titled her article “What a waste! It’s Shakespeare for the kids” and accused Cumberbatch of flattening the famous Danish prince and the production of distilling ”three hours’ worth of philosophy into a Nat King Cole cliché.“

Star of BBC’s Jonathan Strange and Mr. Norrell, Eddie Marsan, tweeted out his disapproval for the premature review.

https://twitter.com/eddiemarsan/status/629219690968162304

While Mark Shenton, lead critic for prominent London theater authority The Stage, told The Hollywood Reporter “a lot will change between the first preview and first night. As critics, it is our job to respect the artistic process; and part of the process is an acknowledgement that they need previews to work on their show before we pass judgment.”

But it’s not just journalist who have proven a little over-eager to share news of Cumberbatch’s high-profile theatrical effort. When addressing a crowd of fans gathered outside the Barbican Centre, Cumberbatch let them know that he had noticed a few overzealous fans filming his performance.

He told the enthusiastic crowd:

I can see red lights in the auditorium, and it may not be any of you here that did that, but it’s blindingly obvious. It’s mortifying, and there’s nothing less supportive or enjoyable as an actor being on stage experiencing that. I can’t give you what I want to give you which is a live performance that you’ll remember — hopefully in your minds and brains whether it’s good, bad, or indifferent — rather than on your phones.

Ever mindful of the power of his social media savvy fan base, however, Cumberbatch did encourage audience members to spread the word of Hamlet on his behalf. “I don’t use social media,” he said, “and I’d really appreciate it if you did tweet, blog, hashtag the shit out of this one for me.” Presumably he hopes all that tweeting and blogging will be text based.

Cumberbatch isn’t the first A-lister to experience leaks in the era of the digitally equipped theatergoer. Both Emma Stone and Jake Gyllenhaal recently saw their musical theater efforts bleed online. And in an internet age where users feel entitled to have everything available to them at all times, the concept of a unique, live theater experience is growing more and more foreign. But the strangest part of all this is that Cumberfans won’t have to wait all that long to be part of the Hamlet experience. On October 15, the production will be broadcast globally as part of the National Theater Live fall programming with Cumberbatch himself saying, “It’s just a great way of making the play accessible.” Hopefully knowing that a high-definition version of Cumberbatch as Hamlet is right around the corner will help answer the eternal question: to leak or not to leak.
