Court Rules That Your Butt-Dials Are Not Private

July 22, 2015 5:15 pm
Suddenly, butts are not that cool anymore.
  • Tina Nguyen

The Sixth Circuit Court of Appeals ruled on Wednesday that people do not have a reasonable expectation of privacy if their butts dial people by accident, making butts the gravest threat to privacy since the Patriot Act. (Maybe not, but who knew that butts could pose a fundamental challenge to the Fourth Amendment?)

The case before the court was a dramatic one: while in Italy, James Huff, the former board chairman of the Cincinnati/Northern Kentucky Airport, accidentally pocket-dialed the assistant to the airport’s C.E.O., Carol Spaw. After attempting to communicate with Huff, Spaw quickly realized that she’d been pocket-dialed (a closely related cousin of the butt-dial), and also realized that Huff was having a conversation, with someone in a hotel in Italy, about firing her boss. Spaw overheard 91 minutes of Huff’s conversation, used an iPhone to record part of it, and shared it with the rest of the board. Huff and his wife, who was also recorded, eventually sued Spaw for violating federal wiretapping laws.

The Sixth Circuit court rejected Huff’s argument that he had a reasonable expectation to privacy, saying that he nullified that right by owning a device that could inadvertently expose his conversations to other parties. (Other examples of the “plain-view” doctrine—which basically means that anything observed lawfully can be obtained and used in court without a warrant—include standing in front of an open window, leaving a webcam on by accident, and putting child pornography on a Napster-like peer-to-peer service. Yes, that happened once.)

However, they pointed out that he could have avoided all of this if he had done the perfectly legal task of locking his phone:

At his deposition, James Huff admitted that he was aware of the risk of making inadvertent pocket-dial calls and had previously made such calls on his cellphone. A number of simple and well-known measures can prevent pocket-dials from occurring. These include locking the phone, setting up a passcode, and using one of many downloadable applications that prevent pocket-dials calls, see, e.g., Will Verduzco, “Prevent Unwanted Butt Dialing with Smart Pocket Guard”… James Huff did not employ any of these measures. He is no different from the person who exposes in-home activities by leaving drapes open or a webcam on and therefore has not exhibited an expectation of privacy.

According to Bloomberg Business, this is the second time in American history that the term “butt dialing” has been printed in a court case, citing a tech law expert who believes that the first case involved a man who inadvertently violated a protective order by butt-dialing the woman he was instructed not to contact.
