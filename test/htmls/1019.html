    'Pixels' Review: Game Over for Adam Sandler

    What could have been a fun romp through 1980s gamer nostalgia is anything but.

    • Share
    • Tweet
    • Pin
    • E-mail
    By Tim Grierson
    Shares
    • Share
    • Tweet
    • Pin
    • E-mail
    • Share
    • Tweet
    • Pin
    • E-mail

    As bad as Adam Sandler's movies have gotten in recent years, they've retained a strain of demented, juvenile strangeness that at least gave them a distinctive air of feverish oddity. Jack and Jill, for instance, is a terrible film, but Al Pacino's go-for-broke cameo as a horny, gonzo version of himself was so astonishingly WTF that it almost redeemed the whole enterprise. With Sandler's latest, though, those obnoxious peculiarities have been scrubbed away, leaving us with just a big, blandly polished bore. Nobody expects much from Sandler's films, but Pixels is a grim reminder that they could always be even worse.

    Advertisement - Continue Reading Below

    Pixels is based on Patrick Jean's clever 2010 short film, which imagined our world being attacked by old-school videogame icons like Pac-Man and Donkey Kong. At two minutes, the original Pixels was a cute, shareable little video that played into adults' nostalgia. But at 100 minutes, this feature adaptation never goes beyond a cool concept, unable to decide if it wants to be Independence Day Lite, a wiseass sci-fi comedy in the vein of Men in Black, or a silly kids movie. In the middle of it is Sandler, looking terribly lost.

    More From Popular Mechanics

    Adam Sandler, the savior of humanity

    The film is directed by Chris Columbus, whose last film debuted five years ago and whose last hits were the first two Harry Potter movies back in the early 21st century. He and screenwriters Tim Herlihy (a longtime Sandler cohort) and Timothy Dowling have tried to dream up a story to justify the short's conceit: In 1982, dorky kid Brenner and his pal Cooper were video game fanatics, but eventually had to leave childish things behind when adulthood beckoned. In the present, Brenner (Sandler) is a lowly worker for a Geek Squad-like company, while Cooper (Kevin James) has become President of the United States. (In a sign of this film's uninspired storytelling, it's never clear whether it's supposed to be funny that a boob like Kevin James has been elected leader of the free world.)

    Underachieving Brenner finds his shot at redemption, however, when aliens invade Earth in the form of '80s video games. Apparently, this alien civilization received a communication we beamed out into the universe 30 years ago that contained our most popular games. The extraterrestrials take this as a declaration of war and, for whatever reason, launch an attack on humanity in the form of Pac-Man, Centipede, Galaga and other games. Because Brenner and his fellow nerds—including conspiracy theorist Ludlow (a gratingly dweeby Josh Gad) and arrogant blowhard Eddie (Peter Dinklage)—are so well-versed in these characters from their childhood spent at the arcade, they alone can save humanity from annihilation.​​

    A typical Sandler comedy is a shaggy, lazy concoction, with the anti-PC jokes almost a dare to incite the easily offended. But although Columbus is nobody's idea of a comic auteur, he brings a professional sheen to this big-budget, effects-heavy action-comedy. That's a change of pace from Sandler's usual lowbrow, low-energy laughers, but it's a change that doesn't pay off. The star shows no signs of being energized by the novelty of working with green screens or a more plot-heavy story. Instead, his general indolence is even more noticeable and off-putting, the actor's small-scale appeal smothered by the film's scope and stakes.

    Game over

    Those kinds of complaints pale in comparison to the fact that Pixels doesn't make sense on the simplest of levels. The aliens come in the form of video game characters, but for some reason, only specific kinds of weapons can defeat them. For example, Pac-Man must be destroyed by hitting him with particularly designed cars that are meant to mimic the game's ghost antagonists. Also inexplicably, the aliens have adopted our old games' three-lives-and-your-done rule, declaring to humanity that whichever side wins three showdowns first will be the champion. If alien races are sophisticated enough to travel to our planet and replicate our video games, surely they could just eradicate us, right?

    Look, I get it—the filmmakers wanted the movie to have these touches of goofy 80s nostalgia, and the fact that they don't make sense wouldn't matter if Pixels were just a goofy lark. But Columbus gives the proceedings a weird sense of dire urgency by insisting that the fate of Earth actually hangs in the balance. Certain supporting characters get pixelated by the alien video game characters, and their agonized screams and slow disintegration suggest they're being painfully killed. By the time we learn that's not the case, it may be too late for your traumatized younger kids. And considering that many of Pixels' jokes involve uncomfortable potshots taken at virginal nerds and career-driven women (namely, Michelle Monaghan as a military advisor who draws the short straw of playing Sandler's contentious love interest), the elevated life-or-death stakes come across as bizarre, almost inappropriate.

    What's even worse, the big set pieces with our heroes squaring off against the likes of Donkey Kong or Centipede are astoundingly unimaginative. What's the point of nostalgia if you can't even evoke the feelings these beloved old games used to spur in us?

    Despite the event-movie slickness surrounding him, Sandler's telltale fingerprints still linger in the margins. Incredibly off-color jokes pop up on occasion (there's a Zapruder film one-liner that's straight-up tasteless) and some of his usual gang of supporting players put in appearances, including Robert Smigel, Nick Swardson, and sportscaster Dan Patrick. But where his films' usual witlessness could provide a modicum of nose-thumbing insolence, Pixels' dull spectacle, lame jokes, and forced romance are so programmatic that it finds the star operating on a whole new level of phoning-it-in. Even Q*bert seems embarrassed having to act alongside him.​​
