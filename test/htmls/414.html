Kids with picky-eating habits have higher rates of anxiety, depression

By Amy Graff on August 4, 2015 1:18 PM
  • 0
  • Print

A new study finds some kids with picky-eating habits have an elevated risk for depression and anxiety. (Anders Andersson/Getty)

Kids commonly protest when anything but butter is put on their pasta. They also tend to throw fits over burgers served with lettuce and tomato or quesadillas stuffed with anything except cheese.

These picky-eating habits are often discounted by today’s pediatricians who advise parents to ignore the behavior.

“It’s a phase,” they say. “He’ll grow out of it.”

“If you fight it,” they warn. “she’ll develop an eating disorder.”

A new study questions this hands-off approach and suggests parents and pediatricians might want to come up with new strategies for supporting those kids who repeatedly refuse to eat what’s being served for dinner.

The study published online today in the journal Pediatrics reveals children who will eat only a limited number of foods are at a higher risk for psychological problems, such as anxiety, depression and attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD), and could benefit from treatment.

The study’s lead author Nancy Zucker of Duke University and her research team looked at 917 children age 2 to 5 years old and found that 20 percent of them were moderately picky eaters. Of those, 3 percent were classified as severely picky, meaning their habits limited them from eating with others.

While the severe picky eaters were more likely to have been formally diagnosed with depression or social anxiety, both the severe and moderate were found to demonstrate elevated rates of behaviors associated with depression, general anxiety and ADHD.

Before you panic over your child who didn’t eat his Brussels sprouts last night, it’s important to know that Zucker didn’t classify misbehaving kids who begrudgingly eat broccoli as picky eaters. Moderate picky eaters were defined as those who refuse to eat anything outside of their preferred range of foods and who frequently have conflicts over food at home, while severely picky eaters were those whose parents were making separate meals for them because they wouldn’t eat what everyone else is having.

“These are children whose eating has become so limited or selective that it’s starting to cause problems,” Zucker, an associate professor of psychiatry and behavioral science at Duke University School of Medicine, said in a press statement. “Impairment can take many different forms. It can affect the child’s health, growth, social functioning, and the parent-child relationship. The child can feel like no one believes them, and parents can feel blamed for the problem.”

Zucker says that some children will grow out of their picky eating habit behavior. For many it’s just a phase. But, she concluded, “Because these children are seeing impairment in their health and well-being now, we need to start developing ways to help these parents and doctors know when and how to intervene.”

Do you have a kid who likes to everything instead of a kid who won’t eat anything? Email your image of your adventuresome eater to moms@sfgate.com with the subject line “My kid ate this.” We’ll feature them in a future photo gallery celebrating kids who will eat more than buttered pasta!
