The Truth About Coffee Is Good News for Caffeine Junkies

Don't let contradicting studies confuse you—we talk to an expert about whether that morning cup of joe is good or bad for you.
BY Jacqueline Burt Cote
  • Print

For those of us who are deliriously, deliciously dependent on coffee, the question of whether or not our daily caffeine fix is actually good for us is moot: We're still gonna keep drinking it. But let's face it, it would be nice to know if the stuff is helping or hurting us—at least then we'd know if we should feel more or less guilty about that third (or fourth) cup. So what's the story?

In recent years, every new study on the benefits or detriments of coffee consumption seems to have canceled out the last (It's good for your heart! it's bad for your brain!). But the latest research offers some good news for the hopelessly java-addicted. For most people, drinking up to six cups of coffee per day doesn't have any serious detrimental health effects, says Rob M. van Dam, Ph.D., coauthor of a Harvard-based study on the association between long-term coffee consumption and increased cardiovascular disease.

"We did not find any relationship between coffee consumption and increased risk of death from any cause, death from cancer, or death from cardiovascular disease," van Dam told Details.

And it gets better: Beyond the revelation that indulging in reasonable amounts of coffee won't hurt you, van Dam says there's strong evidence to support the idea that drinking coffee can even help to protect against certain health issues. Knocking back three to five small cups a day, for example, has been linked to a slightly lower chance of developing cardiovascular diseases; coffee consumption has also been found to lessen diabetes risk. It's worth noting, however, that researchers aren't sure if it's actually caffeine at work in the case of diabetes. "Similar results were observed for caffeinated and decaffeinated coffee, suggesting that compounds other than caffeine may lower risk of diabetes," says van Dam.

Coffee has also been shown to improve laboratory test results in those at risk for liver disease; for patients already suffering from liver disease, it's associated with a slower progression of cirrhosis. (It might be something of a leap to say that's why the first thing you reach for when a hangover hits is a Venti drip, but who knows?)

Does this encouraging information serve as license to guzzle French roast through a funnel? Well, that depends. Pregnant women are advised to limit their coffee consumption to one cup per day. And if you have high cholesterol, you might want to stick to coffee that's been made with a paper filter. That's because coffee contains a substance called cafestol, a stimulator of LDL-cholesterol levels found in the oily fraction of coffee; when you brew coffee with a paper filter, the cafestol gets left behind, explains van Dam.

Still, for most healthy individuals, most signs are pointing to a simple take-away: "I think the evidence is good that for people in general, coffee is one of the healthy beverage choices," says van Dam. So drink up!
