Phones Might Help Blind New Yorkers Navigate the Subway

Travel
  • Mail
  • Tweet
  • Reddit
  • Share
  • like

Lantern, a project from Eugene Gao, would use an app and bluetooth beacons to guide the blind from station to station

Teo Armus
  • 29 june 2015
  • Travel

Navigating New York’s subway system is a challenge—even more so if you’re blind. But a recent project, Lantern, could help blind New Yorkers get around underground with a little more ease.

Dreamed up by New York-based copywriter Eugene Gao as part of a class on mobile advertising, Lantern would harness the power of bluetooth beacons scattered around stations to track the location of smartphones.

Gao tells PSFK that the idea occurred to him while on the subway, after he read about the San Francisco International Airport testing a beacon navigation system. He says:

I was waiting for a downtown F train when I noticed a blind passenger finding his way around the platform and I thought to myself, “It must be so hard to take the subway if you’re blind, it’s confusing for the able-sighted as is.” I closed my eyes, taking in the sounds, smells, and I tried to imagine what it’d be like to ride the subway blind. The thought scared me.

So Gao combined both of his thoughts to create Lantern. In his system, these small beacons—about the size of a bottlecap—would be scattered around subway stations and use battery power to send off information to smartphones.

When an individual using a Lantern app gets within range, information is sent to the app to guide users through the subway and keep them safe. As the user nears stairs or walls, the app would use voice notifications to alert them or let out a high-pitched noise if they’re too close to the edge of a subway platform.

Gao says that Lantern was also inspired by a teacher he had at the Miami Ad School of New York:

His love for tech-driven ideas and experiences left a big influence on me. He waxed lyrical about the potential of beacon technology, and that set me on the path to thinking about using beacons to help people in need.

After a user sets a location, Lantern would provide them with turn-by-turn audio directions to guide them into and through the subway.

Through the use of screen reading technology, the project’s mobile app could provide train schedules and real-time updates upon a voice command from the user.

Gao says he hopes Lantern can encourage blind New Yorkers to use the subway and feel empowered to lead life how they want to. He adds:

It will transform the way the blind will get around the subway and make it significantly easier for them to do so, and even safer. After all, New York City is one of the nation’s most accessible cities, shouldn’t the subway reflect this culture of inclusiveness too?
