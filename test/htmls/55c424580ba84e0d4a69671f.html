Sheila Kim › Elvis Is in the Building: Flavor Paper’s Warhol Collection, Part Deux

From regal Queen Mum to cheery flowers, beloved silkscreen imagery by Andy Warhol made an easy transition into the wall-coverings market when, two years ago, Flavor Paper launched its Andy Warhol x Flavor Paper collection. Initially, it comprised the aforementioned along with other best-known works such as Shoes and The Last Supper. Following that success, the Brooklyn-based wallpaper studio expanded the line this year with Glamericana, a grouping that reimagines the glamorous subjects of Warhol’s.

Add To Collection Save this image to a collection

Elvi, shown in three colorways.

The King, for one, is rendered in two different designs: Elvi sports a repeating pattern of Elvis Presley’s portrait and can be specified in Blue Suede, Fire Red, Graphite, or White. Each is hand-screened onto matte clay-coated paper with traditional paper backing. The large-scale Elvis, meanwhile, is a seven-foot-tall mural showcasing Warhol’s portrayal of gunslinger Elvis. To pack even more visual punch, this design is printed onto a glittery Silver Flash wall fabric.

Add To Collection Save this image to a collection

From left: Marilyn Monoprint shown in Diamond Dust with Hot Pink Blend. Marilyn’s Lips in Gray Fade.

Marilyn Monroe makes an appearance in this second installment, as well. Marilyn Monoprint is a large-scale portrait pattern available in four blended-color inks on mylar. The smaller-scale Marilyn Reversal is a single color print offered in four colorways. And, finally, Marilyn’s Lips showcase, well, the actress’s lips for a sultry result that might induce some to nosh on Twizzlers.

Add To Collection Save this image to a collection

Selfie

But the man behind the iconic works steals the spotlight in the aptly named Selfie. Reproductions of the numerous Polaroid snapshots the artist took of himself are collaged into a mural. Selfie is printed onto eco-friendly, PVC-free EZ Papes, which is pre-pasted with a water-activated adhesive for faster installation. Additionally, the 24-inch-wide wallpaper is water strippable for easy removal and minimal damage to walls. Because this covering is slightly transparent, Flavor Paper recommends priming walls white prior to installation.

Cozy Up To Rich Brilliant Willing's Cocoon Lamps

Brooklyn-based studio Rich Brilliant Willing have a particular skill in turning the once-cold glow of LEDs into warm, inviting household appliances. This year at the International Contemporary Furniture Fair, the trio returned to unveil a new set of zen-like lighting fixtures. Catching Architizer's attention was Mori, a pendant lamp with a unique fabrication process. Its flexible shade consists of a sculpted wire frame continuously wrapped in thread until a “cocoon” (which is why it gets its name from the Bombyx mori silkmoth) forms. And while it’s reminiscent of the spray-coated shade of the classic steel-frame 1960 Gatto Table Lamp by the Castiglionis, it’s a method actually used by the military to secure volatile explosives. It’s then finished with a coat of lacquer. Despite its army-inspired production methods, the finished product is a lot more similar to the Castiglioni lamp, a translucent surface that softly diffuses the light of the bulb inside. Unlike the hot air-balloon-shaped Castiglioni lamp, however, Mori’s concave surfaces look slightly more elegant, and feature an LED inside, of course.

Are 3D Partitions the Future of Wallpaper?

Between the subversive patterns of Studio Job’s new NLXL wallpaper and Flavorpaper’s wall-sized renditions of iconic Andy Warhol works, this season is full of bold wall coverings for the more adventurous. Yet even bolder than wallpaper, we discovered, are the sculptural wall coverings of Vancouver’s Keiou Design Lab, which literally add a new dimension to interiors. Keiou designs and manufactures a line of digitally created, 3D surface patterns molded from a fiber-reinforced plastic that is suitable for indoor or outdoor use. These patterns can make partitions, space dividers assembled from modular blocks, and wall designs, which are continuous relief panels that can be mounted on existing walls with a simple tile adhesive. Both are customizable and primed for painting, giving owners a wide range of options as far as the pattern’s depth and level of privacy — while there are solid patterns, there are also those punctuated with apertures of various sizes for adjustable transparency. They also play quite well with lighting fixtures, which, when embedded into the designs themselves, add yet another layer of depth. Images via Keiou

NLXL's Sinister Wallpapers by Studio Job Win at ICFF

Dutch wallpaper company NLXL took the title for best Wall Coverings this year during the International Contemporary Furniture Fair Awards, thanks to its collaboration with Antwerp-and-Amsterdam-based iconoclasts Studio Job. The design duo, otherwise known as Job Smeets and Nynke Tynagel, created a series of six highly intricate drawings dotted with skulls, skeletons, dying plantlife, and more, that were then digitally permuted to never repeat, no matter how long the pattern continues. For avid art buyers wary of how this densely-patterned paper is going to affect their collection, Smeets offers a modest explanation: "The plain white wall behind a painting is an idea from modernism. In the 17th and 18th centuries, it was not uncommon to present art on a very ornamented silk wallpaper," he explains. "But let me put it another way: with this wallpaper you no longer need any paintings. Viewed in this way — per running meter — it is relatively cheap art." How Hurricane Sandy Gave Birth To Brooklyn's Artiest Wallpaper Company

When Hurricane Sandy struck New York City, Rachel Mosler and Nick Cope were in their apartment in Red Hook. From their abode perched above the beloved Fairway Market, they surveyed the devastating damage the storm wrecked on their neighborhood. They couldn't leave their apartment for a week. With plenty of time to spare as the floodwaters receded, Rachel (a local artist and former art therapist at NYU Hospital) and her husband Nick (the owner of design/build firm Dark Green), along with Nick's studio assistant Najee Wilson, began brainstorming a new project. After countless hours spent experimenting in the studio, the trio finally struck gold. Behold, Calico Wallpaper, a series of luminous large-scale handmade marbleized wallpapers inspired by an obscure 1940s print by the designers Erwine and Estelle Laverne. Now You See It, Now You Don't: Mirrored Furniture That Disappears Before Your Eyes

Visible/Invisible, a furniture series by Brooklyn-based designer Takeshi Miyakawa, alternates between solidity and nothingness depending on where you stand. The pieces—chairs and a table—are composed of mirror-clad acrylic that is then bent and distorted by heat. When positioned just the right way, the mirrored furniture seems to disappear. The series, shown by Salon 94 during New York Design Week, is not Miyakawa's first foray into design. The artist has worked in Rafael Viñoly's model shop for the past two decades, and was arrested last year for leaving LED-filled I [heart] NY bags around Brooklyn.
