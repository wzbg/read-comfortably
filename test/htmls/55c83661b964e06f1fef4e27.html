BuzzFeed Television? Maybe it's closer than you think

2.3k
Shares
Share
Tweet
Share
What's This?
By Jason Abbruzzese2015-08-02 11:00:33 UTC

Arianna Huffington built The Huffington Post into a global digital media empire, while Jonah Peretti, who helped found HuffPo, went on to co-found BuzzFeed, turned into a digital juggernaut that could soon be valued at $1.5 billion. The former colleagues now stand at the vanguard of the new media industry, watched and discussed and speculated about all the time for clues about where digital media is going.

But both of digital media's big empire-builders are looking in another direction: Television.

See also: Vice and BuzzFeed: How Very Different Companies Came to Play the Same Game

Digital media companies, the great disruptors of news and entertainment, are cozying up to television as they look to find cash to justify their lofty valuations. Following in the footsteps of their rapidly growing rival Vice — which made its name on well-produced videos on transgressive subjects and tyrannical regimes — BuzzFeed and more recently HuffPo have begun building out video operations that are producing the kind of original, high-production work that could help tap the big bucks that TV still commands.

The medium of tomorrow? Not exactly. But TV is still very relevant today. Digital media companies have already been ramping up production of video that can appear appear online. Creating a competitive rush are platforms like Facebook and Snapchat, which media companies rely on to popularize their news and features. The problem: social media companies prefer not to push traffic to other websites but instead trap video within their borders. That's sending digital media companies casting a distinct eye on the lucrative TV market.

Ironically, one of the appeals of television is that it has remained resistant to digital media's attempts to kill it. Netflix? Hulu? YouTube? Bring it on. TV channels are still cash cows thanks to the dual income from advertising and affiliate fees, which is the money paid by subscribers and then split among the channels. Those subscribers are in decline, but very slowly. SNL Kagan found that the U.S. pay-TV industry in 2013 declined for the first time by 0.2%.

Not exactly a mass exodus. TV still thrives.

"Despite all the obituaries about television, television is still going very strong," Huffington, president and cofounder of HuffPo, told Mashable.

With that strength comes money.

The enthusiasm and investment in new companies in the past couple years has recently given way to the realization that digital media — turning clicks into cash — is a tough business. Upstarts and veterans alike have struggled to find enough traffic and revenue to turn much of a profit. HuffPo, one of the oldest and largest of the digital media phenoms, reportedly only breaks even.

And the big venture capitalists who backed the digital media hotshots are tapping their feet impatiently to see some results on their generous investments. Huffington Post recently garnered some speculation that it could be worth $1 billion. BuzzFeed has raised $96 million from investors, with rumors that it may get another $250 million.

You can see why TV, with its rich paydays, beckons.

"Most of these guys have investors," said Brian Solis, digital analyst at Altimeter Group. "Once you put money in, you want a return, and television promises a big return."

Must see TV

Here's something else television promises: growth. Digital media companies are finding it difficult to keep delivering the huge online audience growth they grew to depend on or promised investors. Meanwhile, pokey old television is still reaching new audiences, especially in parts of the globe where the internet is spotty or expensive.

Yes, U.S. subscribers are in decline, but when the rest of the world is taken into account, the TV industry is projected to grow. ABI Research foresees 1.1 billion subscribers by 2020 generating a whopping $313 billion. That meshes with the ambitions of BuzzFeed and HuffPo, both of which have made investments in courting overseas audiences.

Meanwhile, new forms of TV are quickly filling the gaps for cord cutters. There's now Comcast's Stream, DishTV's Sling and even standalone options like HBO Now and CBS All Access.

"The idea of TV is bigger than its ever been," Solis said.

TV guide

In March, the Huffington Post announced the creation of HuffPost Studios, a new arm that would be making a variety of video content including documentaries and scripted shows. Much of that video will appear on the website, but it's also meant for broader distribution.

That comes after BuzzFeed CEO Peretti said his company's massive video efforts are headed "upstream," with TV well in the mix. The company also is in talks to raise $250 million as it continues to invest in video production.

They follow in the footsteps of Vice, which has built out its online video operation and expanded into television. Its recently expanded its deal with HBO to include a daily newscast, and will soon take over its own channel thanks to an investment from A+E Networks.

Along the way, Vice grew to be valued at $2.5 billion.

While Peretti said BuzzFeed wasn't quit interested in having its own network, Huffington did not rule it out when asked by Mashable.

"We are exploring everything. We are not ruling anything out," she said.

TV everywhere

Jonathan Nelson, CEO of Omnicom Digital, said that the entertainment industry has taken notice, with many talent agencies now dedicating entire departments to digital video.

HBO is a prime example, he said. Vice will soon provide the pay-TV leader with a daily show as well as more digital-exclusive video for its growing online audience.

"The HBOs are looking for content providers a la Vice," he said. "[Los Angeles] is waking up to this... It's really just beginning out there, but it is definitely beginning."

These kinds of relationships offer digital media companies an important lifeline — cash to please investors and fuel development in the short term as they look to become the major media outlets of the future.

Stay tuned.
